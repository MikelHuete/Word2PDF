Title
What is Artificial Intelligence (AI)?
Heading 2
Purpose of the Lesson
Normal
After this lesson, you will understand the fundamental definition of Artificial Intelligence, the core elements that make AI 'intelligent', and how AI differs from simple automation.
Heading 2
1. The Definition of AI
Normal
Artificial Intelligence (AI)B is a field of computer science that focuses on creating systems or machines that can perform tasks that, if done by humans, would require intelligence. It can be seen as the science and engineering of creating intelligent machines, specifically intelligent computer programs.
Normal
In short, AI is about making computers so 'smart' that they are able to perceive their environment, reason about information, learn from experiences, and make decisions or take actions.
Normal
Not all AI systems perform all of these functions at the same time. Some only perceive and reason, while others may also learn or act.
Normal
Analogy for beginners:B Think about how to teach a dog to play fetch. You throw the ball and encourage the dog to bring it back. After a few attempts, the dog learns what you want. AI systems learn from examples and experiences in a similar way, but instead of rewards, they use data and algorithms.
Heading 2
2. The Core Elements of AI
Normal
To understand the definition, we look at the most important characteristics:
Heading 3
A. Simulation of Intelligence
Normal
AI aims to replicate or simulate aspects of human intelligence. This includes abilities such as:
Normal
Recognizing patterns.
Normal
Troubleshooting.
Normal
Understanding language.
Normal
Learning from experience.
Normal
Making decisions or recommendations.
Normal
AI simulates these abilities through mathematical models and algorithms, not through human understanding or consciousness.
Heading 3
B. Task performance
Normal
The tasks that AI systems perform range from simple (such as sorting emails) to very complex (such as diagnosing illnesses or driving). What makes these tasks 'intelligent' is that they traditionally require human expertise or brainpower.
Normal
AI systems are usually designed for specific tasks rather than general human-like intelligence.
Heading 3
C. Autonomy and adaptation
Normal
A core component of AI is its ability to operate with a certain degree of autonomyB. This means that the system can make choices or take actions without being explicitly programmed for every possible scenario. Additionally, AI systems can adapt, improving their performance as they gain more experience or process more data.
Normal
The level of autonomy varies widely, from fully human-controlled systems to systems that act independently within defined boundaries.
Heading 2
3. How a System Becomes 'Intelligent'
Normal
A system is considered to have artificial intelligence if it can perform the following four fundamental steps:
Normal
Perceive:B Collect information from the environment, for example via sensors, microphones or cameras.
Normal
Reasoning:B Processing and interpreting that information to draw conclusions or make decisions.
Normal
Learn: ImproveB performance over time by learning from data or experience.
Normal
Act:B Taking actions or making recommendations based on reasoning.
Normal
Not every AI system includes all four steps; many systems focus on only a subset.
Heading 2
4. The Role of Data and Algorithms
Normal
AI relies on two main components:
Normal
Data:B Information from the world that is used for learning.
Normal
Algorithms:B Sets of instructions or rules that are used to process that data, recognize patterns, and make predictions or decisions.
Normal
AI uses mathematical formulas and algorithms that use statistics and probability to mimic cognitive functions such as decision-making.
Normal
AI is an umbrella term that includes fields such as Machine Learning (ML)B and Deep Learning (DL):B
Normal
Machine Learning:B Enables machines to learn from data and improve their performance, leading to data analysis and optimization. This learning process often consists of a training phaseB (where the model learns from existing data) and an evaluation phaseB (where the model has to classify new data to achieve the desired result).
Normal
Deep Learning:B This is inspired by the functioning of the human brain,biological neural structures and implemented using artificial neural networks.These networks are powerful at processing and interpreting vast amounts of complex data, which is necessary for tasks such as classification, prediction, and image processing.
Heading 2
5. What AI is NOT (Key Misconceptions)
Normal
To avoid confusion, it's important to know what AI is not:
Normal
Not Just Automation:B Simple automation (like a washing machine that runs through a fixed cycle) is not AI. AI requires a certain amount of 'thinking', where the system analyses, learns or adapts.
Normal
Non-Human Intelligence:B AI processes information and makes decisions based on rules and data. AI does not 'feel' or 'think' like a human, it lacks emotions and intuition, consciousness, and self-awareness.
Normal
AI intelligence is functional, not experiential.
Heading 2
6. AI in Everyday Life (Examples)
Normal
You probably interact with AI on a daily basis without realizing it:
Normal
Imagine an AI system as an extremely organized, high-speed librarian. That librarian has read billions of books (data) and, thanks to complex indexes (algorithms), can quickly find the right information, discover patterns in what people read, and autonomously decide which new book to recommend to you based on your history, but the librarian doesn't 'feel' joy in finding the perfect book.
Normal
This illustrates both the power and the limits of Artificial Intelligence.
Normal
Artificial Intelligence is about data-driven systems that can perceive, reason, learn, and act. It is not human intelligence, but it enables machines to solve complex problems at scale.
